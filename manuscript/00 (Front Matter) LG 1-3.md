## Author's Preface

This book began with a simple question: what does law do to governance?

Lawyers often analyze rights, incentives, and remedies with great precision. They do not always analyze the institutions that make cooperation possible over time. This book argues that governance is one of those institutions and that legal analysis should treat it as a distinct object of inquiry.

I wrote this book for readers in several fields at once. Some will come from corporate law, some from administrative law, some from nonprofit law, and some from institutional economics or commons scholarship. The argument asks those readers to use a shared vocabulary for a problem they often approach through separate traditions.

The book proceeds in three Parts. Part I defines governance and identifies its legal conditions. Part II develops the theory of governance as a club good, distinguishes member benefits from spillovers, and presents the method the book uses. Part III applies that method across a series of institutional domains.

A short note on style may help. I use "governance" in a precise sense throughout the book: the organized system by which a group manages a shared problem over time. I do not use the word as a loose synonym for regulation, management, administration, or social order. Much of the argument depends on keeping those distinctions clear.

Many people contributed ideas, criticism, and encouragement to this project. I am deeply grateful to them. Formal thanks belong in the acknowledgments that follow.
